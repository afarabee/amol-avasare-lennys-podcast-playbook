# Best Practices

## 1. Treat activation as the highest-leverage early growth problem
In AI products, day-zero and day-one experience matter more because capability overhang is real. Product capability is moving faster than user understanding.

## 2. Quality is growth work
When a key journey is visibly rough, stop trying to out-clever the problem with experiments. Fix the experience.

## 3. Use friction when it teaches, routes, or reassures
Bad friction is pointless. Good friction helps the product understand the user or helps the user understand the product.

## 4. In AI-native products, lean harder into larger bets
If the product’s future value could be orders of magnitude larger, don’t let measurable tiny wins dominate strategy.

## 5. Automate loops, not isolated steps
The value is not just AI writing copy. The value is AI helping identify, build, review, and learn from changes.

## 6. Cross-functional alignment remains a hard bottleneck
AI can increasingly help identify likely misalignment, but larger work still depends on real coordination.

## 7. Keep process proportional
Most work should move through the lightest mechanism that prevents expensive confusion.

## 8. Publish judgment, not just decisions
Visible leadership reasoning scales culture, accelerates onboarding, and improves AI usefulness.

## 9. Build around unfair advantages
Double down on the specific intersections where you are unusually strong.

## 10. Re-test what AI can do
Do not assume prior failure is still true after the next model release.

## 11. Leave money on the table when needed
Trust, safety, quality, and brand are not “soft” tradeoffs. They are durable strategic assets.

## 12. Use constraints to sharpen focus
A general technology still needs a narrow wedge. Constraints can free a team from fake optionality.
