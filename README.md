# Amol Avasare: Lenny’s Podcast Playbook

A structured playbook of actionable takeaways distilled from Lenny Rachitsky’s podcast episode with Amol Avasare.

https://youtu.be/k-H4nsOTuxU?si=d9YUkqiJ97jLeeg9

## Why this repo exists

That episode was packed with useful ideas, and I wanted to turn it into something more operational than a page of notes.

So I took the transcript and used AI to extract the most actionable insights, repeatable behaviors, strategic frameworks, and best practices, then organized them into a reusable playbook.

The ideas here are **not** my original frameworks. This repo is my attempt to **organize and operationalize** the most practical takeaways from Lenny Rachitsky’s interview with Amol Avasare into a format that is easier to apply in real product, growth, and AI operating environments.

## What’s inside

This repo is organized into three layers:

### `/skills`
These are action-triggered, reusable skills.

A file lives in `/skills` only if it has:
- a clear, actionable trigger
- a repeatable workflow
- a predictable output format
- a shape that makes sense to invoke at runtime

These are the most operational parts of the repo.

### `/frameworks`
These are structured strategic lenses.

They’re useful for decision-making and analysis, but they are not as cleanly triggerable as runtime skills. Think of these as reusable ways to reason through harder strategic questions.

### `/doctrine`
These are operating beliefs, heuristics, and decision principles.

They’re not standalone execution modules. They’re the judgment layer that should inform how the skills and frameworks get used.

## Themes that came through most strongly

A few ideas showed up over and over again in the episode:

- activation matters a lot more in AI products than many teams realize
- the best onboarding is not always the shortest onboarding
- quality itself can be a growth lever
- AI can help identify misalignment and coordination risk earlier
- process should match the size and risk of the work
- not everything valuable belongs in a formal PRD
- AI-native products may need a different balance between micro-optimizations and bigger bets
- teams need reusable guardrails, not just vague standards
- strong operators revisit their assumptions as tools improve
- good judgment sometimes means deliberately leaving money on the table

## How I approached this

I wanted to be careful not to blur the line between:
- **what sounds smart**
- **what is actually reusable**
- **what deserves to be treated like a real skill**
- **what is better categorized as a framework or doctrine**

So the repo separates material into skills, frameworks, and doctrine based on how reusable and action-triggered it actually is.

That distinction matters to me because a real skill should be something you can actually invoke in response to a concrete need.
